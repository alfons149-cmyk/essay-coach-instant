Reaching C2 means reaching the summit of English writing – not just knowing how to build sentences, but how to shape ideas with elegance and conviction.

At this level, writing is not about following formulas. It is about thinking clearly, writing fluidly, and expressing complex ideas with accuracy and style.

In this unit, you will explore the C2 essay task in detail: what the exam demands, how it differs from C1, and how to plan, develop, and write with the maturity and control of a near-native academic writer.

1. The Nature of the C2 Essay Task

In the Cambridge Proficiency (CPE) exam, Part 1 of the Writing paper is always an essay based on two short texts (often articles, reports, or extracts).

You must:

  • read the texts carefully,
  • identify their main ideas,
  • compare or evaluate them, and
  • present a well-argued response based on your own opinion.

The task typically asks you to summarise and evaluate the key points, compare perspectives, and add your own argument – in about 240–280 words.

Sample Task

You have read two extracts about the role of risk in people’s lives.
Write an essay summarising and evaluating the key points from both texts.
Use your own ideas to develop your response.

This may sound similar to C1, but at C2 the difference lies in depth and flexibility. Examiners are looking for your ability to:

  • think critically,
  • connect ideas across texts,
  • express nuanced conclusions with confidence.

2. What Examiners Are Looking For

At the C2 level, all four marking criteria still apply, but they are assessed with higher expectations.

Criterion What it means at C2
Content You fully understand, summarise, and evaluate both texts accurately.
Communicative Achievement Your essay is academic, fluent, and persuasive without being mechanical.
Organisation Paragraphs flow naturally; ideas are connected logically and stylistically.
Language Range, flexibility, and precision – near-native command of grammar and vocabulary.

In short:
C1 rewards control.
C2 rewards sophistication.

3. The Key Difference Between C1 and C2 Essays

Compare the two levels side by side:

Element C1 (Advanced) C2 (Proficiency)
Task Type Discuss two opinions and give your view. Summarise, evaluate, and develop ideas from two texts.
Focus Clear argument, balanced reasoning. Depth, insight, and synthesis of ideas.
Language Accurate, formal, and controlled. Flexible, natural, and stylistically mature.
Tone Academic, calm, confident. Analytical, refined, subtly persuasive.
Word Count 220–260 words. 240–280 words.

So while the C1 essay shows logical control, the C2 essay shows an intellectual voice – how you think about ideas, not just describe them.

4. Reading and Understanding the Source Texts

At this level, your first task is not to write – it is to read intelligently.

When you read the two short texts, focus on four questions:

  • What are the main ideas of each?
  • What assumptions or attitudes do they reveal?
  • Where do they agree or disagree?
  • How do they relate to the question?

Example texts

Text A:
“Taking risks is necessary for personal growth. People who avoid risk often fail to discover their potential.”

Text B:
“Modern society glorifies risk-taking, yet most success comes from patience and consistency rather than impulsive decisions.”

From this, you can see:

  • Both discuss risk, but from opposite perspectives.
  • Text A values boldness; Text B values stability.

A strong essay will summarise both, then evaluate the tension between them.

5. Planning a C2 Essay

Planning is shorter but sharper at this level. Your goal: clarity of structure, flexibility of thought.

Recommended outline

  • Introduction – summarise the topic and mention both texts (≈ 40–50 words).
  • Body paragraph 1 – discuss and evaluate Text A (≈ 60–70 words).
  • Body paragraph 2 – discuss and evaluate Text B, with some comparison (≈ 60–70 words).
  • Conclusion – integrate ideas and give your final, balanced view (≈ 50–60 words).

C2 essays must feel coherent but not formulaic. Your plan gives you direction, but your thinking adds life.

6. Writing an Elegant Introduction

Your introduction should summarise the issue raised in both texts – briefly, neutrally, and clearly.

Example

Both texts explore the idea of risk as a defining factor in human progress. While the first emphasises the value of taking chances to achieve growth, the second questions this assumption, suggesting that stability may be a more reliable path to success.

✔ Simple, factual, and fluent.
✖ No filler phrases like “In this essay I will discuss…”
You start with ideas, not with instructions.

7. Analysing and Evaluating (Not Just Summarising)

C2 examiners look for evaluation – your ability to respond to ideas, not just describe them.

Summarising = restating what the text says.
Evaluating = analysing why it matters or how convincing it is.

Example

Text A claims that risk is essential for personal development. This argument is persuasive, as individuals often learn more from challenges than from comfort. However, it overlooks the potential cost of failure, which may discourage some people from taking meaningful risks.

Notice how you include:

  • a short summary (what the text says),
  • evaluation (your analysis),
  • balance (strengths and limitations).

That is exactly what earns top marks at C2 – not a long summary, but a short, thoughtful one.

8. Linking and Synthesis Between Texts

You must also link the two texts – show how they relate.

Example of synthesis

Both writers consider the relationship between risk and achievement, yet they interpret it differently. Whereas the first associates risk with progress, the second connects it with instability and wasted potential.

The word whereas immediately signals comparison. Your essay now reads like a single coherent response, not two separate summaries.

9. Developing Your Own Argument

After discussing both texts, move naturally into your own perspective. This is where your academic voice takes centre stage.

Example

In my view, taking risks is valuable only when supported by preparation and self-awareness. Blind courage may lead to short-term excitement, but true success requires calculated decision-making and resilience.

This type of writing shows:

  • independence,
  • reflection,
  • stylistic control.

It is no longer only about grammar; it is about judgement.

10. Using Sophisticated Linking Phrases

At the C2 level, you must go beyond standard connectors (however, in addition, therefore). Your transitions should sound natural and flexible.

Function Useful phrases
Contrast whereas, while, on the contrary, by contrast, despite this
Cause / Effect consequently, thus, hence, as a result, this leads to
Evaluation this argument appears convincing, this claim seems less persuasive
Balance although, even though, on the one hand… on the other hand
Summarising overall, ultimately, all things considered, in essence

Example in context

While the argument in the first text is compelling, it risks oversimplifying the complex nature of decision-making in modern life.

Smooth, academic, and natural – exactly the tone of C2.

11. Maintaining a Natural, Academic Tone

At this level, avoid both extremes:

  • Too formal: It is incontrovertibly evident that…
  • Too casual: I think people should just be brave.

Aim for elegant simplicity:

Risk-taking can drive innovation, but it also requires judgment and balance.

Short, clear sentences with strong nouns and verbs create authority. C2 writing feels confident – never rushed, never artificial.

12. Model C2 Essay (≈ 260 Words)

Task

Summarise and evaluate the key points from both texts about risk-taking.

Model Answer

Both texts discuss the value of taking risks, but from contrasting perspectives. The first argues that risk is essential for growth, suggesting that individuals who avoid challenges also limit their potential. The second, however, views risk more critically, pointing out that stability and long-term commitment are often more effective routes to success.

The first text’s optimistic view is persuasive, as progress in any field requires stepping beyond one’s comfort zone. Without risk, innovation would stagnate, and personal development would be limited. Nevertheless, it overlooks the fact that not all risk is productive; failure can be discouraging, and recklessness may cause long-term harm.

The second text offers a valuable reminder that caution and persistence often lead to sustainable results. Its argument is balanced, though perhaps too cautious, as it underestimates the role of boldness in driving change.

Ultimately, both texts highlight essential aspects of human progress. In my view, risk is most valuable when combined with preparation and reflection. People should neither avoid it completely nor pursue it blindly, but approach it as a necessary ingredient for growth, managed through self-awareness and responsibility.

Why this works:

  • ✔ Clear summary of both texts.
  • ✔ Balanced evaluation.
  • ✔ Mature personal viewpoint.
  • ✔ Controlled, formal tone.
  • ✔ Fluent, varied language.

This is what a Band 4–5 (C2-level) essay looks like.

13. Common C2 Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Over-summarising

Mistake: simply restating what both texts say.
Fix: add evaluation and your own insight.

Over-formality

Mistake: It is irrefutably undeniable that…
Fix: simplify to It is clear that…

Lack of synthesis

Mistake: treating each text separately.
Fix: compare and link them throughout your essay.

Over-opinionated tone

Mistake: The second text is wrong.
Fix: The second text’s argument seems less convincing.

Ignoring the word limit

Mistake: writing 320+ words.
Fix: stay within 240–280; clarity is more important than quantity.

Your goal is balance – depth without heaviness, elegance without pretension.

14. Developing C2-Level Precision

At this level, small word choices make a big difference.

Basic Advanced
think argue / maintain / contend
show illustrate / reveal / demonstrate
say claim / state / suggest
people individuals / society / the public
problem issue / challenge / dilemma
good beneficial / valuable / constructive
bad harmful / counterproductive / problematic

Editing these choices into your essay gives it C2 polish.

15. Final Reflection

Mastering the C2 essay means learning to think and write with quiet sophistication. It is not about more vocabulary or longer sentences.

It is about precision, connection, and mature reasoning.

At this level:

  • you do not just answer the question – you explore it,
  • you do not just argue – you evaluate,
  • you do not just write – you communicate thought.

Remember:
B2 builds structure.
C1 builds control.
C2 builds insight.

So when you sit the C2 exam, do not write to impress – write to express. Trust your preparation, think calmly, and let your ideas flow in clear, confident English.

12. Summary & Quick Reference

Unit 19 showed you how to approach the C2 essay task, where the goal is not just to write an opinion piece but to analyse, integrate, and evaluate ideas from the texts. Use this summary as a quick reminder of what the examiner expects at the highest level.

✔ What the C2 Essay Really Tests

  • Understanding — showing the key points of each text accurately.
  • Integration — combining ideas from both texts logically.
  • Evaluation — assessing strengths, weaknesses, assumptions, and implications.
  • Position — presenting a clear, well-justified viewpoint.
  • Academic control — tone, structure, cohesion, precision.

Weak (descriptive):

Text A says that social media is harmful. Text B says it has benefits.

Strong (evaluative + integrative):

While Text A highlights psychological risks associated with excessive social media use, Text B convincingly argues that these risks depend largely on how the platforms are used — a distinction that Text A does not fully address.

✔ How to Integrate the Texts Smoothly

At C2 level, paragraphs should blend ideas—never treat the texts separately.

Useful integration frames:

  • Text A argues…, while Text B suggests…
  • Both texts highlight…, but they differ in…
  • Text B builds on Text A’s point by…
  • Although Text A criticises…, it does not consider… explored in Text B.

Separated:

Text A mentions the lack of regulation. Text B focuses on personal responsibility.

Integrated:

Although Text A attributes online misinformation to weak regulation, Text B shifts responsibility toward individual users, implying a more decentralised solution.

✔ Evaluating Arguments: What C2 Examiners Look For

You must show what each text gets right, overlooks, or assumes.

Evaluate by checking:

  • Strengths: clarity, evidence, logic, relevance.
  • Weaknesses: exaggeration, missing evidence, weak assumptions.
  • Balance: does the text acknowledge complexity?
  • Implications: what follows if the argument is accepted?

Minimal evaluation:

Text A gives good reasons, but it is too negative.

Strong evaluation:

Text A provides convincing evidence through recent statistical studies, but its conclusion assumes that increased screen time automatically leads to lower wellbeing — an assumption not fully supported by the data.

✔ Showing a Strong, C2-Level Position

Your opinion should be: clear, well-argued, and built on the texts—not personal stories.

Effective ways to express your position:

  • I contend that…
  • This essay argues that…
  • Although Text A raises valid concerns, I agree with Text B that…
  • The evidence presented suggests that…

Weak:

I agree with Text A because I’ve seen this happen in my own life.

Strong:

Although Text A highlights legitimate risks, Text B presents a more balanced view, offering a solution that addresses both personal responsibility and systemic challenges.

✔ Quick C2 Essay Structure (Perfect for Exam Day)

  • Intro: summarise both texts objectively + state your approach.
  • Body 1: key idea → integrate both texts → evaluate.
  • Body 2: another key idea → integrate → evaluate → lead to your position.
  • Conclusion: clear final judgement, rooted in both texts.

✔ C2 Quick Checklist

  • Have I integrated the texts in every paragraph?
  • Have I evaluated ideas, not just described them?
  • Have I avoided personal stories/opinions?
  • Is my argument balanced and insightful?
  • Is the tone academic, precise, and confident?

✔ Try It Yourself

Rewrite this at C2 level (integrate + evaluate):

  Text A says city life is stressful.
  Text B says cities offer better opportunities.

Targets:
  • Show the relationship between ideas
  • Evaluate strengths/weaknesses
  • Provide a clear, academic tone

At C2, you are not only a writer — you are an analyst.
