Unit 11

Editing for Accuracy

Useful for fixing:

  • a / an / the (articles)
  • uncountable nouns
  • if-clauses / conditionals
  • who / which / that (relative pronouns)
  • spelling of key words

You have planned, written, and organised your essay. Now comes the step many students rush – or skip completely: editing.

Good writers do not stop when they finish the last sentence. They pause, breathe, and check. Editing is not about perfection; it is about clarity. It is the moment you turn good writing into confident, controlled writing – the kind that Cambridge examiners immediately trust.

1. Why Editing Matters

Even excellent ideas lose power when they are hidden under small mistakes. Grammar slips, missing commas, or repeated words can make a strong essay look careless.

Editing is your chance to catch those small errors before the examiner does. It is also a way to show maturity: you do not panic; you check your work like a professional.

Writing shows your ideas. Editing shows your control.

Cambridge examiners value accuracy highly. A student who writes clearly, even with simple language, often scores higher than one who uses advanced words incorrectly.

2. How Much Time to Spend

You have about five minutes at the end of your essay for editing. That is enough – if you use it well.

Those five minutes can easily improve your mark by half a band, sometimes even a full band. Plan your timing like this:

  • 5–7 minutes to plan
  • 25 minutes to write
  • 5 minutes to check and polish

The goal is not to rewrite everything, but to correct what weakens your essay.

3. The Four Levels of Editing

To edit efficiently, follow this order:

  1. Content and organisation – Is everything clear and complete?
  2. Grammar and sentence structure – Are tenses and agreement correct?
  3. Vocabulary and word choice – Are words appropriate, precise, and formal?
  4. Punctuation and spelling – Are commas, full stops, and capitals correct?

We will go through each level one by one.

4. Level 1: Content and Organisation

Before checking small details, make sure your essay answers the task. Ask yourself:

  • Did I include all three notes from the question?
  • Did I give my opinion clearly?
  • Does each paragraph have one clear idea?
  • Does the essay flow logically from beginning to end?

Example of a missing note

Mistake: An essay about health that covers diet and stress but forgets your own idea.

Fix: Add a short extra paragraph:

Another important factor is getting enough rest. Sleep helps the body recover and stay strong.

Fixing content is more important than fixing commas. If your essay does not answer the full question, your score drops immediately – no matter how beautiful the grammar is.

5. Level 2: Grammar and Sentence Structure

Now zoom in. Look for the mistakes that lower your accuracy marks: verb forms, agreement, articles, and sentence control.

5.1 Verb tense

Check that all verbs match the time you mean:

  • Last year → past
  • now / these days → present
  • in the future → will / going to

5.2 Subject–verb agreement

Does the verb match the subject?

✅ People like online shopping.
❌ People likes online shopping.

5.3 Articles: a / an / the

Articles are small but important. Use a / an for general, non-specific things, and the for specific things you and the reader both know.

✅ She is a teacher.
✅ The teacher in my class is very strict.
❌ She is teacher.

When editing, quickly scan for nouns and ask: “Do I need a/an here? Or the?”

5.4 Countable and uncountable nouns

Some nouns are uncountable in English (e.g. information, advice, homework). They do not take -s in the singular.

❌ Many informations.
✅ A lot of information.
✅ Many pieces of information.

When editing, circle any uncountable nouns and check that you have not used a plural form.

5.5 Sentence length and structure

Are any sentences too long or joined incorrectly? Split long, confusing sentences into two clearer ones.

❌ I think exercise is important it helps people stay healthy.
✅ I think exercise is important. It helps people stay healthy.

5.6 Relative clauses (who / which / that)

Use who for people, which for things, and that for both in many informal cases.

✅ People who recycle help the planet.
✅ The programme which / that I watched was very interesting.

When editing, check each who/which/that: does it refer to the right noun?

5.7 If-clauses and conditionals

Conditional sentences have two parts: the if-clause and the result clause. Make sure the tense combination is correct.

✅ If people exercised more, they would feel healthier. (second conditional)
✅ If more people use public transport, cities will become less polluted. (first conditional)

When editing, quickly scan for “if” and check that the tenses on both sides match the pattern you want.

5.8 Listening to your sentences

When editing, read slowly and “aloud in your head”. Your ear often catches mistakes that your eyes miss.

❌ People enjoys doing exercise because it make them relax.
✅ People enjoy doing exercise because it makes them relax.

6. Level 3: Vocabulary and Word Choice

Next, look at your word choices. Are they appropriate for a Cambridge essay? Formal writing does not need difficult words – just the right ones.

6.1 Avoid informal or spoken words

❌ Kids these days don’t care about recycling.
✅ Many young people today do not care enough about recycling.

6.2 Replace “very + adjective”

❌ very good → ✅ excellent
❌ very bad → ✅ terrible
❌ very important → ✅ essential / crucial

6.3 Add variety instead of repetition

❌ important… important… important…
✅ important… essential… significant…

6.4 Check collocations (natural word combinations)

❌ make a research → ✅ do / conduct research
❌ strong rain → ✅ heavy rain

6.5 Avoid vague words

❌ things, stuff, a lot of, nice
✅ issues, materials, many, beneficial

Each time you replace a vague or informal word with a precise one, you show control. That is what examiners mean by “range of vocabulary”.

7. Level 4: Punctuation and Spelling

Now look at the technical side of writing. Punctuation does not only mark pauses – it shows your reader how to read your ideas.

7.1 Full stops

Every sentence must end with one complete idea.

❌ I think exercise is important it helps people stay healthy.
✅ I think exercise is important. It helps people stay healthy.

7.2 Commas

Use commas to separate clauses or after linking phrases.

✅ In my opinion, public transport should be free.
✅ Although it is expensive, many people still drive to work.

Avoid overusing commas – one per sentence is often enough in exam writing.

7.3 Capital letters

✅ The River Thames flows through London.

7.4 Spelling of key words

Some common tricky words:

  • environment ✅ (not enviroment)
  • government ✅ (not goverment)
  • necessary ✅ (two s, one c)
  • definitely ✅ (not definately)
  • people ✅ (not peoples, unless you mean nations)
  • their / there / they’re → choose carefully

In the exam, you cannot use a spellchecker. Practise writing key words by hand – it trains your memory.

8. How to Check Efficiently in Five Minutes

Here is a calm, systematic approach for your final five minutes:

  1. Paragraph 1 (Introduction): Does it answer the question? Is your opinion clear? No contractions (don’t, can’t)?
  2. Paragraphs 2–3 (Body): Does each paragraph have one clear idea? Are linking words used naturally? Is grammar consistent?
  3. Paragraph 4 (Conclusion): Clear summary and final opinion? No new information?
  4. Whole essay: Word count roughly 140–190? No obvious spelling or punctuation errors?

Use your pencil to underline or lightly mark errors. Then correct them neatly – legibility matters.

9. Editing Tricks for Speed

Even when time is short, these small techniques make editing faster and smarter.

9.1 Read backwards, sentence by sentence

Start from the last sentence and move up. This breaks the “flow” of reading and helps you notice grammar or spelling mistakes.

9.2 Use your finger or pencil to guide your eyes

Move slowly under each line. Your brain slows down and pays more attention.

9.3 Circle linking words

If you find too many and / but / because, replace one or two with more advanced connectors such as although, therefore, however.

9.4 Highlight verbs mentally

Check if they all agree with the subject and stay in the same tense. Small corrections here have a big effect.

10. The Most Common B2 Errors (and Quick Fixes)

Articles

❌ She is teacher.
✅ She is a teacher.

Wrong plural of uncountable nouns

❌ Many advices.
✅ Much advice / many pieces of advice.

Prepositions

❌ Depend of → ✅ Depend on

Tense and if-clauses

❌ Last year I go to London. → ✅ Last year I went to London.
(Check past forms carefully.)

Word order

❌ Always I study English. → ✅ I always study English.

Double subject

❌ My brother he likes football. → ✅ My brother likes football.

Run-on sentences

❌ I was tired I went home. → ✅ I was tired, so I went home.

Missing -s in present simple

❌ He work hard. → ✅ He works hard.

Make this list part of your pre-exam revision. If you fix just two or three of these during editing, your accuracy score rises noticeably.

11. Self-Correction Strategies (Before the Exam)

Editing is easier in the exam if you practise it during preparation. Try these habits:

11.1 Keep an error notebook

Write down your most frequent mistakes and the correct forms.

❌ people likes → ✅ people like
❌ I am agree → ✅ I agree

Look through your notebook before every writing practice.

11.2 Use colour when editing drafts

At home, highlight grammar issues in one colour, vocabulary in another, and connectors in a third. This trains your eyes to see patterns in your mistakes.

11.3 Compare your writing to model answers

Notice how the model uses transitions, punctuation, and tone. Ask: “Does my version sound too casual or too mechanical?”

11.4 Time yourself

Practise editing under real exam conditions. Try reading and correcting your essay in exactly five minutes.

12. Balancing Accuracy and Expression

Accuracy is vital, but do not let it stop your flow. Some students become so afraid of making mistakes that they write very slowly, using only safe words. That limits your Language Range score.

The balance is this: in the exam, write freely first – then edit carefully. Your first draft shows your creativity. Your edits show your control. Together, they make you sound confident and natural.

13. Practice: Edit Like an Examiner

Try this short exercise.

Student version

People today is very busy, they hasn’t time to cook. Because of this they eat fastfood, it is bad for health. The goverment should do something.

Step-by-step edit

  1. Grammar:
    People today are very busy; they do not have time to cook.
  2. Punctuation:
    Join correctly: Because of this, they eat fast food, which is bad for their health.
  3. Spelling:
    goverment → government.
  4. Style:
    More formal: The government should take action to improve eating habits.

Edited version:

People today are very busy and often do not have time to cook. As a result, they eat fast food, which is bad for their health. The government should take action to improve eating habits.

This is the kind of calm, corrected writing examiners love – clear, controlled, and accurate.

14. What Examiners Notice When You Edit

Cambridge examiners read quickly, but they can tell when a student has edited their work. They notice:

  • clear punctuation and paragraph spacing,
  • few repeated errors (shows awareness),
  • balanced tone and consistent tenses,
  • neat corrections (not messy crossings-out).

Editing does not just clean your essay – it communicates professionalism.

15. Final Reflection

Editing is the quiet strength behind every good writer. It is not dramatic, but it is powerful. It is the final handshake between your ideas and your reader.

Planning gives structure.
Writing gives meaning.
Editing gives quality.

When you edit, you say: “I respect my writing enough to polish it.” That respect shows in every line. Before you put down your pen in the exam, breathe. Read your essay once more. Listen to its rhythm. Fix what sounds wrong. Trust your instincts.
