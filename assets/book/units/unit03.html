Unit 3

Types of Sentences and Connectors

By now, you know what clauses are and how basic word order works in English. You can build clear sentences with the subject, verb, and extra information in the right place. In this unit, we go one step further: you’ll learn how to combine ideas and connect them naturally.

When you master sentence types and connectors, your writing stops sounding like a list of short statements and begins to feel like real communication. That is exactly what Cambridge examiners want to read.

1. Why Sentence Variety Matters

Look at this short paragraph:

I like my job. I work with friendly people. I learn something new every day. I sometimes get tired. I want to improve.

Every sentence is correct, but the effect is flat. The rhythm never changes, so the reader quickly loses interest.

Now try this version:

I like my job because I work with great people, and I learn something new every day. Although I sometimes feel tired, I still want to improve.

The ideas are the same, but the writing is more natural. Clauses are connected, and the sentence length varies. That is the power of sentence variety.

In this unit we will explore four main sentence types and the connectors that help you create this natural flow.

2. The Four Types of Sentences

There are only four basic sentence types, but together they give you everything you need for Cambridge exam writing.

a) Simple Sentence

A simple sentence has one independent clause. It may have more than one verb, but it expresses a single complete idea.

Examples

  • I enjoy reading, speaking in public, and eating out.
  • My teacher is kind and friendly.
  • The meeting starts at ten.

Think of a simple sentence as a short breath. It is clear, direct, and confident. In essays, simple sentences are useful for emphasis or after longer structures to create rhythm:

Education shapes our society and determines its progress. Everyone deserves that chance.

b) Compound Sentence

A compound sentence joins two independent clauses (two complete sentences) with a connector. Both parts are equally important.

There are three main ways to build them:

  1. With a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS)
    for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so
    • I studied hard, and I passed the exam.
    • She wanted to go out, but it was raining.
    • We didn’t have enough time, so we skipped dessert.
  2. With a semicolon (;)
    • I worked very hard on my essay; it went really well.
    • The bar is empty now; it’s usually crowded on weekends.

    The semicolon creates a strong link and puts subtle emphasis on the second clause. Used well, this feels advanced and controlled.

  3. With a conjunctive adverb (we’ll explore these in detail soon)
    • I wanted to go swimming; however, I was too tired.
    • He didn’t study; therefore, he failed the exam.

Compound sentences are ideal when you want to show balance between two ideas, especially for contrast or cause and effect in essays.

c) Complex Sentence

A complex sentence contains one independent clause plus one or more dependent clauses (extra ideas that cannot stand alone).

Dependent clauses often begin with subordinating conjunctions such as because, although, when, if, unless, while, since, before, after.

Examples

  • Because it was late, we went home.
  • Although I was tired, I finished my homework.
  • I’ll call you when I arrive.
  • We stayed inside while it was raining.

Complex sentences allow you to show logical relationships such as:

  • Cause – because, since
  • Contrast – although, even though
  • Time – when, while, after
  • Condition – if, unless

These are essential for clear, analytical writing in Cambridge exams.

d) Compound–Complex Sentence

A compound–complex sentence combines everything: it has at least two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

Examples

  • Because I had a headache, I went home, and I took an aspirin.
  • Although it was raining, we decided to go out, and we had a great time.
  • I love studying English because it’s useful, and it helps me express my ideas better.

These appear frequently at C1 and C2 level. They show you can manage several ideas in one sentence without losing clarity – a strong sign of advanced control.

3. Understanding Connectors

Now that you know the sentence types, you need the tools that link them: connectors (also called linking words).

Connectors tell the reader how one idea relates to the next: reason, contrast, result, sequence, example, and so on. Without them, writing feels like separate pieces instead of one coherent message.

We’ll look at three main groups:

  1. Coordinating conjunctions
  2. Conjunctive adverbs
  3. Subordinating conjunctions

4. Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

You probably already know these: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so. They link two ideas of equal importance in a compound sentence.

Connector Function Example
for reason We stayed home, for it was raining.
and addition He cooked dinner, and he washed the dishes.
nor negative addition He didn’t call, nor did he send a message.
but contrast I wanted to go out, but I was tired.
or alternative You can take the bus, or you can walk.
yet contrast (similar to “but”) The test was challenging, yet I passed.
so result It started raining, so we stayed inside.

Notice the inversion after nor in the example: nor did he send… – this is typical and sounds very natural at B2–C2 levels.

Comma rule: use a comma before these words when they join two independent clauses:

✅ I studied all night, but I still didn’t pass.

5. Conjunctive Adverbs

Conjunctive adverbs are more formal linking words, ideal for essays and reports. They usually appear:

  • after a semicolon ; in the middle of a sentence, or
  • at the beginning of a new sentence

In both cases, they are typically followed by a comma.

Common conjunctive adverbs

  • Adding information: moreover, furthermore, in addition, additionally
  • Showing contrast: however, nevertheless, on the other hand, in contrast
  • Showing result: therefore, thus, consequently, accordingly
  • Examples / emphasis: for example, indeed, in fact, certainly
  • Time / sequence: then, meanwhile, afterwards, subsequently

Examples in context

  • The course was challenging; however, I learned a lot.
  • The project was successful; therefore, the manager congratulated the team.
  • We studied hard; in fact, we reviewed every topic twice.
  • He started working at nine; meanwhile, I was still sleeping.

These connectors sound academic and elegant. They help you avoid repeating “and” or “but” and give your writing a more professional tone.

6. Subordinating Conjunctions

Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses and show a clear relationship between ideas.

Common groups

  • Cause / Reason: because, since, as
    I stayed at home because I was sick.
  • Contrast / Concession: although, even though, whereas, while
    Although it was cold, we went for a walk.
  • Time: when, while, after, before, until, since
    We left after we finished dinner.
  • Condition: if, unless, provided that, as long as
    You can go out if you finish your homework.
  • Purpose / Result: so that, in order that
    She spoke clearly so that everyone could understand.

These words are essential for expressing logic, which is exactly what examiners look for in your essays.

Example in an essay

Although online learning is convenient, it can never replace real human interaction because students need face-to-face communication to develop social skills.

Here, although introduces contrast and because introduces reason – two layers of logic in one sentence.

7. Common Mistakes with Connectors

Let’s correct some typical learner problems.

❌ Because I was tired. I went home.
✅ Because I was tired, I went home.

The dependent clause Because I was tired cannot stand alone. It must be connected to a main clause.

❌ I studied hard, however I failed the test.
✅ I studied hard; however, I failed the test.

A conjunctive adverb like however needs a stronger break (semicolon or full stop) before it.

❌ Although it was raining but we went out.
✅ Although it was raining, we went out.

Never use although and but together. Choose one connector, not both.

8. Sentence Variety in Action

Compare these two versions.

Version 1 – flat

Technology is changing education. Students use computers. Teachers use online platforms. Exams are also digital. Many people think it’s a good change.

Version 2 – improved

Technology is changing education. Students now use computers, and teachers organise lessons through online platforms. Although some people worry about screen time, digital tools make learning faster and more flexible. As a result, many students see this as a positive change.

What changed?

  • Sentence length and structure now vary.
  • Connectors show the relationships between ideas: although, as a result.
  • The writing feels like English, not like a list of separate comments.

This is the effect you want in your own essays.

9. Practice: Identify and Create

Task 1 – Identify the sentence type

Decide if each sentence is simple, compound, complex, or compound–complex.

  1. I enjoy reading English novels.
  2. I studied hard, but I didn’t pass.
  3. Because I was tired, I went to bed early.
  4. Although I was tired, I cooked dinner, and I watched TV.

Task 2 – Complete the sentence with a connector

  1. I didn’t have breakfast, ______ I wasn’t hungry.
  2. We finished our project; ______, the teacher was very pleased.
  3. ______ it was raining, we decided to go out.

Task 3 – Combine ideas

Join each pair into a clear, flowing sentence using any appropriate connector.

  • I wanted to call you. My phone battery died.
  • She loves writing. She practises every day.
  • The exam is next week. We still have time to prepare.

Example answer: I wanted to call you, but my phone battery died.

10. Bringing It Together

Although learning English writing takes time, it can be an enjoyable process if you understand how sentences work. First, you need to master the structure of clauses and phrases. Then, you can connect your ideas using conjunctions and linking words. As a result, your essays will not only sound more natural but will also show examiners that you control the language. Writing well is not about long words — it is about clear, connected sentences that express ideas logically.

In that short paragraph, you can see:

  • complex and compound–complex sentences
  • a range of connectors (although, if, then, as a result)
  • a natural rhythm and clear progression of ideas

That is exactly the effect Cambridge examiners look for. In the next unit, we will build on this foundation by exploring punctuation and sentence flow — how commas, semicolons, and full stops bring even more control and elegance to your writing.

Sentences are like people – they work best when they connect.
