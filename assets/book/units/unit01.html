Unit 1

Clauses, Phrases, and Sentences

Before we can write clear, elegant essays, we must first understand what English writing is built on — the sentence. Every sentence has a structure, and that structure depends on clauses and phrases. Think of them as the building blocks of meaning. Once you know how to use these blocks, you can build anything — from a short, simple sentence to a long, beautifully connected paragraph.

1. What Is a Clause?

A clause is a group of words that contains a subject and a verb. That’s the key:

subject + verb = clause

For example:

  • I play tennis every Saturday.
  • She is reading a book.
  • We went to the beach.

All three are complete thoughts because they tell us who is doing something and what they are doing. These are called independent clauses because they can stand alone.

Now look at these examples:

  • Because I studied hard
  • When it stopped raining
  • Although I wasn’t hungry

They also contain a subject and a verb, but they don’t express a complete idea — you are left waiting for more information. These are dependent clauses because they rely on another clause to complete the meaning.

Examples:

  • Because I studied hard, I passed my exam.
  • When it stopped raining, we went outside.
  • Although I wasn’t hungry, I ate a slice of cake.

Tip: A clause is like a small sentence that sometimes needs a friend.

2. Types of Clauses

Independent Clause (IC)

  • Can stand alone
  • Expresses a complete thought
  • Always has a subject + verb

Example: I love studying English.

Dependent Clause (DC)

  • Cannot stand alone
  • Starts with a subordinating word: because, although, when, if, unless, while…
  • Needs another clause to complete meaning

Example: Because I love studying English, I spend time writing every day.

3. What Is a Phrase?

A phrase is a group of words that has meaning but does not contain both a subject and a verb. It cannot stand alone as a sentence.

Examples:

  • The big blue house at the end of the road
  • In the middle of the night
  • To find a better job
  • With great enthusiasm

A phrase adds colour to a sentence, but it cannot be the sentence itself.

Phrases Inside Sentences

Phrase Sentence Example
At the end of the road There is a shop at the end of the road.
To find a better job She moved to London to find a better job.
With great enthusiasm The students greeted their teacher with great enthusiasm.

Each phrase adds meaning — about place, purpose, or manner — but the core sentence still depends on the clause (subject + verb).

4. What Is a Sentence?

A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. It must contain:

  • a subject
  • a verb
  • a complete idea

A correct sentence always:

  • Starts with a capital letter
  • Ends with a full stop, question mark, or exclamation mark

Examples

  • I play tennis every Saturday.
  • She loves writing essays.

Incorrect: Because I studied hard.

Corrected: Because I studied hard, I passed my exam.

5. Understanding Sentence Patterns

English usually follows this pattern:

Subject + Verb + Object + (Extra Information)

Example:

I ate my pizza quickly in the car last night because I was in a hurry.

  • Subject: I
  • Verb: ate
  • Object: my pizza
  • Manner: quickly
  • Location: in the car
  • Time: last night
  • Purpose: because I was in a hurry

Good vs. unnatural word order:

  • Yesterday, I ate my pizza… ✔
  • I yesterday ate my pizza… ✘

6. Two Basic Sentence Types in English

(a) Verbs of Motion

go, come, drive, fly, walk, run, travel, move

Example:

I went to the city centre by bus this morning to buy myself a new shirt.

  • Who: I
  • Verb: went
  • Where: to the city centre
  • How: by bus
  • When: this morning
  • Why: to buy myself a new shirt

(b) No-Motion Verbs

eat, make, study, watch, have, enjoy, write

Example: I ate my pizza quickly in the car last night…

Pattern:

Subject → Verb → Object → Manner → Location → Time → Purpose

7. Types of Sentences

To write advanced Cambridge essays, you must use a variety of sentence types.

a) Simple Sentence

One independent clause.

  • I study English every day.
  • The exam starts at nine o’clock.

b) Compound Sentence

Two independent clauses joined by a connector.

  • I studied hard, and I passed the exam.
  • The weather was bad, but we still went for a walk.

c) Complex Sentence

One independent clause + one dependent clause.

Example: Because it was my birthday, we ate out at a restaurant.

d) Compound–Complex Sentence

At least two independent clauses + one dependent clause.

Example: Because I had a headache, I went home, and I took an aspirin.

8. Connecting Your Clauses

You can join clauses using:

  • Coordinating conjunctions: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so
  • Conjunctive adverbs: however, therefore, moreover, nevertheless, meanwhile
  • Subordinating conjunctions: because, although, when, unless, while, since, if…

9. Putting It All Together

Example paragraph:

In England, there are speed limits clearly displayed on the roads to show the maximum speed you should drive. Some drivers just don’t seem to notice these, and they treat every trip as a race, overtaking in dangerous places like on a blind bend. The speed limits are there for a reason, and it’s always worrying to see people driving like this.

This paragraph uses a mix of simple, compound, and complex sentences, plus precise phrases to add detail.

10. Quick Practice

  1. Identify the clause type:
    a) When I was young, I loved reading fairy tales.
    b) I love reading fairy tales.
    c) Because I love fairy tales.
  2. Complete the sentence:
    Although it was raining… → Although it was raining, we decided to go out.
  3. Combine:
    I was tired. I finished my homework. → Although I was tired, I finished my homework.

11. Final Thoughts

You’ve just taken the first step toward mastering essay writing. Understanding clauses, phrases, and sentences is like learning how to build a house. If the foundations are strong, everything else — paragraphs, essays, and even exam tasks — becomes easier.
