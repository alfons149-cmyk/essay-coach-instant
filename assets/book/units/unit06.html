Unit 6

Word Choice and Vocabulary Precision

Once your sentences are grammatically correct, the next step is to make them sound natural and convincing. The difference between a simple B2 text and a strong C1/C2 essay is often not grammar, but word choice.

In this unit, we will focus on choosing words that are accurate, precise, and appropriate for Cambridge exams. We will look at:

  • why word choice matters for higher bands
  • how to avoid weak words like very
  • how to use adjectives, collocations, and verbs naturally
  • how to choose formal alternatives and the right register
  • how to avoid wordiness and choose synonyms correctly

By the end of this unit, you will be able to make your writing clearer, more advanced, and more “native-like” – exactly what Cambridge examiners reward at higher levels.

1. Why Word Choice Matters

Look at these two sentences:

  • People are angry about the situation.
  • People are furious about the situation.

Both are correct, but the second one is stronger, more natural, and more advanced. It uses one precise word (furious) instead of two weaker ones (very angry).

Cambridge examiners sometimes write: “Good control of vocabulary but limited range.” This means your words are mostly correct, but not varied or rich enough.

To reach higher bands (B2 → C1 → C2), you need to show:

  • range – a variety of appropriate words
  • precision – words that express your exact meaning

In the rest of this unit, you will see concrete techniques to expand both your range and your precision.

2. Avoid Weak Intensifiers like “Very”

Students often use very when they do not know a stronger word. However, very usually makes your writing weaker, not stronger.

2.1 Replace “very” with a stronger word

Instead of: very + adjective, choose a single, stronger adjective.

Very + adjective Stronger alternative(s)
very bigenormous, huge, massive
very smalltiny, minute
very hotboiling, scorching
very coldfreezing
very tiredexhausted, weary, drained
very happydelighted, thrilled
very sadmiserable, heartbroken
very angryfurious, enraged, livid
very goodexcellent, outstanding, impressive
very badawful, terrible, dreadful

Example:

❌ The weather was very hot, and people were very tired.
✅ The weather was scorching, and people were exhausted.

The second sentence sounds more vivid and confident – exactly the tone you want in a Cambridge essay.

3. Gradable and Non-Gradable Adjectives

Not all adjectives behave in the same way. Some are “measurable”; others are absolute. This affects which adverbs you can use with them.

3.1 Gradable adjectives

Gradable adjectives describe qualities that can be stronger or weaker. You can be a bit tired, quite tired, or very tired.

They work with: a bit, quite, very, rather, extremely

Examples:

  • She is quite tired today.
  • I am very cold.
  • They were extremely happy with the results.

3.2 Non-gradable adjectives

Non-gradable adjectives describe absolute states or extremes. You cannot be “a bit dead” or “very perfect”.

They work with: absolutely, completely, totally, utterly

Examples:

  • She was absolutely exhausted after the exam.
  • The film was utterly boring.
  • His explanation was completely wrong.

Common mistake:

❌ very exhausted
✅ absolutely exhausted

This may feel like a small detail, but examiners notice it. Correct use of gradable and non-gradable adjectives shows advanced control of vocabulary.

4. Natural Word Partnerships (Collocations)

A collocation is a natural combination of words that frequently appear together. Native speakers do not usually say do a photo or make an exam – they say:

  • take a photo
  • take an exam

In Cambridge essays, good collocations make your writing sound fluent and natural.

4.1 Useful verb collocations for essays

  • Take: a photo, an exam, action, responsibility, part in
  • Do: research, homework, business, a favour
  • Make: a decision, a difference, progress, an effort, a mistake
  • Give: advice, a presentation, permission, a reason
  • Pay: attention, a visit, respect, a fine, the price
  • Have: experience, difficulty, an impact, an effect

Examples:

  • We need to take action to reduce pollution.
  • She made an effort to arrive on time.
  • The teacher gave advice about improving grammar.

Learning collocations is one of the most efficient ways to sound more advanced in writing.

5. Strong Collocations for Style

At higher levels, you can use stronger, more idiomatic collocations instead of “very + adjective”.

5.1 Common strong collocations

  • bitterly disappointed, cold, opposed
  • deeply ashamed, grateful, concerned, moved
  • highly successful, unlikely, qualified, recommended
  • totally / completely different, alone, honest, unacceptable
  • seriously damaged, ill, worried, affected
  • utterly useless, impossible, exhausted

Example:

❌ People were very disappointed with the results.
✅ People were bitterly disappointed with the results.

These combinations sound much more natural and “native-like” than simply using very.

6. Strong Verbs vs. Weak Verbs

Weak verbs like do, make, get, have often need extra words to carry the meaning. Strong verbs are more precise and formal – ideal for academic writing.

6.1 Replace weak verb phrases

Weak expression Stronger verb
make betterimprove
make worseworsen, damage
make a decisiondecide
get betterrecover, improve
get worsedecline, deteriorate
have an influence oninfluence, affect
give helpassist, support
do researchconduct research
do an examtake, sit an exam
put into practiceapply

Examples:

❌ The government must make better rules.
✅ The government must improve its regulations.

❌ We did research about this topic.
✅ We conducted research on this topic.

Using strong verbs makes your writing more concise, formal, and professional.

7. Formal Alternatives to Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs (such as put off, give up, come up with) are very common in spoken English. In formal Cambridge writing (especially at C1 and C2), it is often better to choose a more formal alternative.

7.1 Useful formal equivalents

Informal / spoken Formal / academic
find outdiscover
give upabandon, quit
come up withpropose, suggest
put offpostpone, delay
go upincrease, rise
go downdecrease, decline
bring aboutcause, lead to
look intoinvestigate, examine
talk aboutdiscuss
deal withaddress, tackle

Examples:

❌ We need to look into this problem.
✅ We need to investigate this problem.

❌ Prices went up a lot last year.
✅ Prices increased significantly last year.

You do not need to eliminate all phrasal verbs – some are perfectly fine in formal writing. The goal is to show that you can choose more formal alternatives when appropriate.

8. Avoiding Wordiness

Good writers say a lot with few words. Wordiness makes your writing heavy and unclear.

Examples:

❌ In today’s modern society of today, people should make an effort to try to reduce the level of pollution that exists.
✅ Today, people should reduce pollution.

❌ It is my opinion that teachers should be paid more.
✅ Teachers should be paid more.

In exams, you have limited time and word count. Clear, direct sentences help the examiner follow your ideas quickly – and this supports a higher mark.

9. Synonyms in Context

Using synonyms is not about choosing the most “advanced” word. It is about choosing the word that fits the context and your exact meaning.

9.1 Useful synonym groups

Basic word Possible alternatives (depending on context)
goodbeneficial, positive, valuable, effective
badharmful, negative, damaging, poor
importantessential, crucial, vital, significant
bigmajor, considerable, substantial
smallminor, slight, limited

Example:

❌ The weather was crucial today. (incorrect – “crucial” means essential, not “nice”)
✅ The meeting was crucial today. (correct – a meeting can be essential)

Precision is more important than trying to impress. A simple, correct word is better than a complex word used in the wrong context.

10. Choosing the Right Register

Every situation has an appropriate level of formality – its register. Cambridge essays are usually formal or semi-formal, so you should avoid slang and very casual expressions.

10.1 Informal vs. neutral/formal

Informal Neutral / formal
a lot ofnumerous, a great deal of
kidschildren
stuff / thingsitems, materials, issues
good jobeffective work, achievement
anywaynevertheless, however, nonetheless
really / veryextremely, highly, particularly
I think / I believeit seems, it appears
because ofdue to, owing to
getobtain, receive
giveprovide, offer

Example:

❌ A lot of kids get bad grades because of difficulties at home.
✅ Many children receive low grades due to domestic issues at home.

Even at B2 level, choosing a slightly more formal register can make your writing look more professional. At C1 and C2, it is essential.

11. Practice: Strengthen Your Vocabulary

Transform these sentences by choosing more precise and formal words.

  • The results were very good.
  • People are very angry about the changes.
  • The problem got worse over time.
  • We talked about pollution in class.
  • The company made a decision to start a new project.

Possible answers:

  • The results were excellent / impressive.
  • People were furious about the changes.
  • The problem deteriorated / worsened over time.
  • We discussed pollution in class.
  • The company decided to start a new project.

Now write 3–5 of your own sentences. Start with a simple version, then rewrite it using:

  • stronger adjectives or adverbs
  • strong verbs instead of weak ones
  • more formal alternatives to phrasal verbs

12. Bringing It All Together

Compare these two versions of the same idea.

Before:

People are very worried about pollution. It is a very big problem in cities. Governments should make better laws and do more research about it.

After:

People are deeply concerned about pollution, which has become a major problem in urban areas. Governments should improve environmental laws and conduct more research to address it effectively.

The second version uses stronger verbs, better collocations, and more precise vocabulary. It sounds like a real Cambridge C1 answer: clear, formal, fluent, and specific.

13. Final Reflection

Words are powerful. The ones you choose tell the reader who you are: careful or careless, precise or vague, confident or unsure.

Precision is not about showing off with difficult words. It is about respect – respect for your reader’s time, for the examiner’s expectations, and for your own ideas.

Good grammar makes your writing correct.
Good word choice makes your writing convincing.
