Unit 6: Word Choice and Vocabulary Precision

Once your sentences are structurally strong, the next step is to make them shine. In Cambridge exams, what separates a good essay from an excellent one is often not grammar, but word choice.

Choosing the right words is like choosing the right colours for a painting: each has a shade, a tone, and an emotional effect. Some words are formal, some casual. Some precise, some vague. This unit helps you choose vocabulary that is accurate, controlled, and naturally suited to Cambridge-style writing.

1. Why Word Choice Matters

Compare these two versions:

  • People are very angry about the situation.
  • People are furious about the situation.

Both are correct — but the second sounds more natural, powerful, and advanced. Cambridge examiners often write comments like:

“Good control of vocabulary but limited range.”

This means your words are right, but not rich enough. To reach C1–C2, you need range and precision.

2. Rule One: Avoid “Very” (and Weak Intensifiers)

Students often use “very” when they don’t know a stronger word. But “very” actually weakens your writing because it shows uncertainty.

Instead of “very…”Use…
very bigenormous / huge / massive
very smalltiny / minute
very hotboiling / scorching
very tiredexhausted / weary / drained
very happydelighted / thrilled
very angryfurious / enraged / livid
very goodexcellent / outstanding / impressive

Example:

❌ The weather was very hot, and people were very tired.
✅ The weather was scorching, and people were exhausted.

A single precise word immediately elevates your writing.

3. Rule Two: Gradable vs Non-Gradable Adjectives

Adjectives come in two categories:

  • Gradable — you can measure them (cold → very cold)
  • Non-gradable — absolute states (perfect, dead, impossible)

Patterns:

  • Gradable → very, a bit, rather, quite, extremely
  • Non-gradable → absolutely, completely, totally, utterly

Examples:

  • She’s absolutely exhausted. (not “very exhausted”)
  • The film was utterly boring.
  • I’m very tired. (gradable)

Small detail, big impact — examiners notice this.

4. Rule Three: Learn Natural Collocations

A collocation is a natural word partnership. Native speakers don’t say do a photo or make an exam.

Common verb collocations:

takea photo, an exam, action, responsibility, part in
doresearch, homework, business, a favour
makea decision, progress, an effort, a mistake
giveadvice, a presentation, permission, a reason
payattention, a visit, respect, a fine
haveexperience, difficulty, an impact

Examples:

  • We need to take action to reduce pollution.
  • She made an effort to arrive on time.
  • The teacher gave advice about improving grammar.

Collocations are one of the fastest ways to sound natural at C1–C2.

5. Rule Four: Use Strong Adverb–Adjective Collocations

These sound natural and advanced:

  • bitterly disappointed/opposed/cold
  • deeply ashamed/grateful/concerned
  • highly successful/unlikely/recommended
  • totally different/unacceptable/honest
  • seriously damaged/ill/worried
  • utterly exhausted/useless/impossible

Example: ❌ People were very disappointed. ✅ People were bitterly disappointed.

6. Rule Five: Choose Verbs That Carry Meaning

Weak verbs: do, make, get, have
Strong verbs: precise, academic, professional.

Weak expressionStronger verb
make betterimprove
get worsedecline / deteriorate
do researchconduct research
make a decisiondecide
put into practiceapply

Examples:

  • ❌ We did research on this topic. ✅ We conducted research on this topic.
  • ❌ The government must make better rules. ✅ The government must improve its regulations.

7. Rule Six: Replace Phrasal Verbs (when needed)

Phrasal verbs are natural in speech, but in Cambridge essays they often sound informal.

Replace with more formal verbs:

InformalFormal
find outdiscover
give upabandon
come up withpropose / suggest
look intoinvestigate / examine
put offpostpone / delay

You don’t need to eliminate all phrasal verbs — just make conscious, formal choices.

8. Rule Seven: Avoid Wordiness

Good writing says a lot with few words.

Examples:

❌ In today’s modern society of today...
✅ Today…

❌ It is my opinion that teachers should be paid more.
✅ Teachers should be paid more.

Cambridge examiners reward clarity and confidence, not filler.

9. Rule Eight: Learn Synonyms by Context

The best synonym is the one that fits the meaning in context — not the one that “sounds advanced.”

  • good → beneficial, positive, effective
  • bad → harmful, damaging
  • important → essential, vital, significant
  • big → major, substantial
  • small → minor, limited, slight

Example:
❌ The weather was crucial today. ✔ The meeting was crucial today.

10. Rule Nine: Choose the Right Register

Cambridge essays are formal or semi-formal. Avoid casual vocabulary.

11. Practice: Strengthen Your Vocabulary

Transform these sentences:

  1. The results were very good.
  2. People are very angry about the changes.
  3. The problem got worse over time.
  4. We talked about pollution in class.
  5. The company made a decision to start a new project.

Possible answers:

  • The results were excellent.
  • People were furious about the changes.
  • The problem deteriorated over time.
  • We discussed pollution in class.
  • The company decided to start a new project.

12. Bringing It All Together

Before:

People are very worried about pollution. It is a very big problem in cities. Governments should make better laws and do more research about it.

After:

People are deeply concerned about pollution, which has become a major issue in urban areas. Governments should improve environmental laws and conduct additional research to address it.

Vocabulary precision transforms your writing into clear, fluent, exam-ready English.

13. Final Reflection

Your word choice tells the examiner who you are as a writer — precise, thoughtful, and in control. Grammar makes your writing correct, but vocabulary makes it convincing.
