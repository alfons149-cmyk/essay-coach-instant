10.1 Showing Your Opinion with the Right Words

In Cambridge essays you must give your opinion, but the way you express it should sound formal and controlled. Instead of repeating “I think…” in every paragraph, use a range of neutral, academic phrases.

  • I think… → In my view… / In my opinion…
  • I really think… → It seems clear that… / It appears that…
  • I am sure… → I am convinced that…

These expressions clearly show your position without sounding emotional or informal, which is exactly what examiners want.

10.2 Covering All Parts of the Task

Vocabulary also helps you show that you have covered all the notes or bullet points in the exam question. Use clear signalling phrases so the examiner can easily see that you answered each part.

  • Firstly, regarding public transport, …
  • Another important aspect is health, …
  • Finally, considering young people’s free time, …

These phrases connect your ideas directly to the task notes. If your essay loses marks for “task achievement”, strengthening these signals is an easy win.

10.3 Vocabulary That Fits the Text Type

Different exam tasks need slightly different vocabulary. An essay usually needs neutral, academic language. A review needs opinion words about books, films, or places. An article can be a bit more personal and engaging.

  • Essay: advantages, disadvantages, moreover, on the other hand, in conclusion
  • Review: entertaining, disappointing, gripping, highly recommended
  • Article: imagine, surprisingly, one of the best ways

When your vocabulary matches the text type, examiners feel that you understand the task, not just the language.
