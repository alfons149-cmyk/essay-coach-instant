Unit 14: Planning and Developing C1 Essays

By now, you already understand what the C1 essay task looks like. You know that you read two short texts, discuss both, and give your own opinion.

But there is one thing that separates strong C1 writers from average ones: planning as structured thinking.

At this level, planning is no longer just listing ideas. It is about structuring thought – deciding what to include, what to leave out, and how to guide the reader through a logical, elegant argument.

This unit will show you how to plan and develop your essay so that your writing feels intelligent, calm, and controlled from start to finish.

1. Why Planning Matters Even More at C1

At B2 level, planning helps you avoid repetition and simple mistakes.

At C1, it does something deeper: it shows critical thinking. Cambridge examiners want more than clear writing – they want reasoned writing.

A well-planned C1 essay shows that you can:

  • analyse different viewpoints,
  • choose your own position,
  • and express your reasoning with flow and confidence.

Without planning, your ideas can feel unbalanced: too much on one side, not enough on the other. With a plan, every paragraph has a job.

Remember:
B2 planning organises ideas. C1 planning organises thinking.

2. Understanding the Source Texts

Before you plan, you must understand what the input texts actually say.

Sample task:

You have been discussing the impact of technology on daily life. Below are some opinions:

  • “Technology has made life more convenient.”
  • “Technology has created more problems than it has solved.”

Write an essay discussing both opinions and give your own view. (220–260 words)

At C1, this is not just a list of advantages and disadvantages. You need to interpret what each opinion implies.

  • “Technology has made life more convenient.” → focuses on comfort, access, speed, communication, information, and efficiency.
  • “Technology has created more problems than it has solved.” → points to side effects such as stress, inequality, dependence, or privacy issues.

Once you see what each idea really means, your plan – and your essay – become more thoughtful.

3. Step One: Break Down the Question

Before you brainstorm, ask yourself three simple questions:

  1. What is the topic?
    Technology in daily life.
  2. What are the two given opinions?
    (a) Technology makes life more convenient.
    (b) Technology creates more problems.
  3. What is my task?
    Discuss both opinions and give my own view.

Now your brain knows exactly what it is dealing with. Everything you write must connect to those three answers.

4. Step Two: Brainstorm Ideas (with Purpose)

At this level, brainstorming is not about writing down every idea you can think of. It’s about choosing ideas that show insight.

Opinion 1: “Technology has made life more convenient.” Opinion 2: “Technology has created more problems.”
  • Faster communication (emails, video calls, messaging apps).
  • Easy access to information and education (online courses, tutorials).
  • Health and fitness apps that support lifestyle changes.
  • Online banking and shopping save time and effort.
  • Dependence on devices; constant distraction.
  • Data privacy issues, online scams, tracking.
  • Social isolation, stress, and comparison via social media.
  • Environmental impact: e-waste, energy consumption.

Now choose one or two ideas from each side that you can develop clearly. At C1, quality is more important than quantity.

5. Step Three: Choose Your Opinion

At the end of your essay, you must give a personal view. The strongest C1 opinions are usually balanced, not extreme.

For example:

  • “Technology brings more advantages than problems, but only if it is used responsibly.”
  • “Technology creates serious problems, yet it is still essential for progress.”

This kind of nuanced opinion sounds mature and earns higher marks. It shows that you can see both sides before reaching a conclusion.

6. Step Four: Create a Logical Outline

Once you have your ideas and your overall view, turn them into a quick outline. Here is a typical C1 structure for our sample task:

Part Content Notes
Intro Introduce topic + both views Technology in daily life; some see it as convenient, others see it as harmful.
Body 1 Discuss “more convenient” Communication, access to information, education. Example: online learning, remote work.
Body 2 Discuss “more problems” Overuse, mental health, privacy, dependence. Example: social media stress, cybercrime.
Conclusion Give your own view Benefits outweigh drawbacks, but people and governments must use technology responsibly.

That outline is your “blueprint”. You have already decided what you are going to say – now the writing will be much smoother.

7. Step Five: Develop Each Idea with Depth

At C1, you don’t just state ideas – you develop and evaluate them. Think of each paragraph as a small argument with a beginning, middle, and end.

You already know the PEEL model. Here we add one more layer, Evaluation:

  • P – Point: State the main idea.
  • E – Example / Evidence: Give a concrete example.
  • E – Explain: Show why it matters.
  • L – Link: Connect to the next idea or opinion.
  • + Evaluation: Add a reflective thought or limitation.

Example (C1 paragraph skeleton):

Point: Technology has made communication significantly easier.

Example: For instance, video calls allow families in different countries to stay in touch every day.

Explain: This saves time and reduces feelings of isolation, making daily life more comfortable.

Link: However, not everyone sees these changes as entirely positive.

Evaluation: Overall, the convenience is undeniable, but it depends on using these tools in a balanced way.

That final evaluation step is what really pushes your writing from B2 to C1. It shows that you can think about your ideas, not just describe them.

8. Step Six: Plan Your Connectors and Transitions

At C1, flow is almost as important as content. Your plan should already include some transitions between paragraphs.

Function Advanced connectors
Adding furthermore, in addition, what is more
Contrasting nevertheless, however, despite this, on the other hand
Result consequently, as a result, thus, therefore
Balancing while it is true that…, even though…, although…
Summarising overall, in summary, to sum up

Example transition plan:

  • Between intro and Body 1: To begin with, many people argue that technology has made everyday life more convenient.
  • Between Body 1 and Body 2: Nevertheless, there is also a strong argument that technology has created new problems.
  • Before the conclusion: Overall, both sides have valid points, but…

You don’t need dozens of connectors. You need a few that you can use naturally and logically.

9. Step Seven: Decide How to Integrate the Two Opinions

At B2, you usually keep ideas separate: one paragraph per point. At C1, you have more flexibility. You can:

  • use a separate approach: one paragraph per opinion, or
  • use an integrated approach: compare both within the same paragraph.

Separate approach (clear and safe):

  • Paragraph 1 – Opinion 1 (advantages)
  • Paragraph 2 – Opinion 2 (problems)
  • Conclusion – your view

Integrated approach (more advanced):

  • Paragraph 1 – Main advantage, with brief contrast
  • Paragraph 2 – Main disadvantage, directly compared to the benefit
  • Conclusion – your view

Integrated sentence example:

Technology has undoubtedly made communication faster and easier; however, this constant connectivity can also increase stress and reduce privacy. The same tool that connects us can, paradoxically, isolate us.

The integrated structure can sound very elegant, but it requires control. Use it once you feel confident with the separate structure.

10. Step Eight: Use the “Balance Scale” Method

Here is a useful mental image: Imagine your essay as a scale. Each side represents one of the opinions.

  • Your job is to keep the scale reasonably balanced while you write.
  • You can lean slightly to one side (your final opinion), but you should not ignore the other side.

Example of imbalance:

  • Paragraph 1 (Opinion 1): 80 words
  • Paragraph 2 (Opinion 2): 30 words

This makes the essay feel one-sided, and examiners will mark it down for weak organisation. Aim for roughly similar length and depth when discussing each opinion.

11. Step Nine: Build Your Own “Idea Bank”

Planning becomes much faster when you already have ideas for common C1 topics. Start a small notebook or digital file where you store 4–6 key ideas per theme.

Topic Typical idea bank notes
Education equality, online learning, creativity vs exams, lifelong learning
Technology convenience, privacy, dependence, innovation
Environment personal responsibility, government policy, green energy
Work job satisfaction, job security, work–life balance, automation
Health mental vs physical health, diet, stress, lifestyle
Society social media, community, individualism, shared values

Before each essay, you only need to adapt ideas from your bank to the specific question. That preparation builds confidence – and speed.

12. Step Ten: Review Your Plan Before Writing

Before you start your first sentence, pause for ten seconds and scan your plan. Ask yourself:

  • ✅ Do I understand both opinions?
  • ✅ Have I chosen a clear view?
  • ✅ Do my main paragraphs feel balanced?
  • ✅ Do I have connectors ready?
  • ✅ Can I reasonably stay within 220–260 words?

If the answer is yes, start writing. Your hand will move more confidently because your brain already knows the path.

13. Common Planning Mistakes at C1

  • Overplanning
    Writing full sentences in your plan and losing time.
    Fix: use short notes and keywords.
  • Ignoring one opinion
    Only writing about benefits or only about problems.
    Fix: always include both as the task requires.
  • Too many ideas
    Trying to mention five or six points per side.
    Fix: choose two strong arguments and develop them well.
  • No clear opinion
    Describing both sides but never giving your own view.
    Fix: make your conclusion clearly state your position.
  • No timing practice
    Planning for 15 minutes in practice, then panicking in the exam.
    Fix: keep planning to about 5–7 minutes and practise that rhythm.

Remember: the goal of planning is direction, not perfection.

14. Model Example: C1 Essay Plan

Task:

Some people say modern technology improves our quality of life. Others say it causes new problems.

5-minute outline:

Intro: Technology transforms daily life; people disagree whether it helps or harms society overall.

Body 1 (Benefits):

  • Communication – faster, global connections.
  • Education – access to online courses, resources.
  • Health – medical technology (remote surgery, digital records).

Body 2 (Problems):

  • Social isolation and addiction (screen time, social media).
  • Privacy and security issues (data collection, cybercrime).
  • Environmental cost (e-waste, energy consumption).

Conclusion: Technology improves life overall, but only if risks are managed responsibly by individuals and society.

Word count target: 230–250 words Tone: formal, analytical, balanced.

15. Final Reflection

At C1 level, a good essay is not about how much you know – it is about how clearly you can show it.

Planning gives you that clarity. It turns your thoughts into structure, your structure into flow, and your flow into confidence.

When you plan carefully:

  • you write faster,
  • you sound more intelligent,
  • you stay calmer under time pressure.

Remember:
Good writers think before they write. Great writers think while they plan.
