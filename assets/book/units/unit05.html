Unit 5

Building Strong Paragraphs

In Cambridge essays, the paragraph is your basic building block. If your paragraphs are clear, focused, and well developed, your whole essay will feel organised and easy to follow.

In this unit, you’ll learn how to build effective paragraphs using topic sentences, supporting ideas, and examples, and how to avoid common problems such as weak starts, random ideas, and underdeveloped points.

1. What Is a Paragraph?

A paragraph is a group of sentences that develop one main idea. In Cambridge essays, each main body paragraph normally:

  • begins with a topic sentence,
  • explains that idea with supporting sentences,
  • adds examples or evidence,
  • sometimes finishes with a short mini-conclusion or link.

If a paragraph has too many different ideas, it feels messy. If it has only one or two short sentences, it feels underdeveloped.

2. Topic Sentences

The topic sentence is usually the first sentence of a paragraph. It tells the reader:

  • what the paragraph is about, and
  • what you want to say about that topic.

Weak topic sentence:

Technology is very important nowadays.

Better topic sentence:

One major advantage of technology in education is that it gives students instant access to information.

The second version is better because it is specific. It prepares the reader for a paragraph about access to information, not about “technology” in general.

Good topic sentences often:

  • include a clear controlling idea (one main focus),
  • avoid vague phrases like “a lot of things” or “some advantages”,
  • match the task and the paragraph’s function (argument, example, explanation).

3. Support and Development

After the topic sentence, you need to develop the idea. This is where many students lose marks: they give a good idea, but they do not explain it or support it.

Think in three layers:

  1. Point – your main idea (topic sentence).
  2. Explanation – why / how / in what way?
  3. Example – a simple, clear illustration.

Point:

One advantage of online learning is flexibility.

Explanation:

Students can choose when to study, which makes it easier to combine courses with work or family responsibilities.

Example:

For instance, many adults take evening webinars after work instead of having to travel to a physical classroom.

Notice how the explanation and example stay focused on the same idea: flexibility in online learning.

4. Unity and Coherence

A strong paragraph has both unity and coherence.

  • Unity = every sentence supports the topic sentence.
  • Coherence = sentences are connected in a logical order.

Paragraph with weak unity:

One advantage of online learning is flexibility. Students can watch lessons when they have time. Some people also think traditional schools should teach more life skills. Online courses are often cheaper.

Improved version:

One advantage of online learning is flexibility. Students can watch lessons when they have time, rather than following a fixed timetable. This makes it easier to combine study with work or family responsibilities. As a result, many adults choose online courses instead of travelling to evening classes.

In the improved version, every sentence supports the same idea and the logic is clear: advantage → explanation → result.

5. Model Exam Paragraph

Task: Some people say that reading books is no longer important because we can get information online. Do you agree?

Model paragraph:

One reason why reading books is still important is that it develops deeper concentration than online texts. When students read a book, they usually focus on one author’s ideas for a longer period of time. This helps them follow an argument from beginning to end and understand the connections between different points. By contrast, when they read short posts online, they often jump quickly from one topic to another, which makes it harder to think in a structured way.

This paragraph has a clear topic sentence, strong development, and a contrast that fits well in Cambridge essays.

6. Practice

Task 1 – Improve the topic sentence

Rewrite these weak topic sentences to make them more specific.

  1. There are many problems with homework.
  2. Technology has some disadvantages.
  3. Sport is important for young people.

Task 2 – Add explanation and example

Complete the paragraph by adding explanation and an example.

Start: One benefit of doing part-time work as a student is that it teaches responsibility.

Task 3 – Check unity

Write a short paragraph (5–7 sentences) about one advantage of social media. Then check:

  • Does every sentence support the topic sentence?
  • Did you include clear explanation and at least one example?

7. Final Thoughts

Strong paragraphs are the skeleton of your essay. If your topic sentences are clear and every paragraph is well developed, examiners can quickly understand your ideas and give you credit for them.

In the next units, we will look more deeply at cohesion and coherence – how to link sentences and paragraphs so that your writing feels smooth and natural from beginning to end.

8. Summary & Quick Reference

Core ideas

  • A paragraph develops one main idea.
  • A good paragraph usually follows: Topic sentence → Explanation → Example → (Mini-conclusion).
  • Unity = all sentences support the topic sentence.
  • Coherence = ideas are in a logical order and clearly connected.

Typical exam problems

  • Paragraphs that are too short (2–3 sentences only).
  • Paragraphs with several different ideas mixed together.
  • No clear topic sentence, so the examiner has to guess the focus.
  • Good ideas but no examples or explanation.

Useful patterns

  • Point: One disadvantage of large cities is the noise.
  • Explanation: Constant traffic and construction make it difficult to relax.
  • Example: For example, many people sleep with earplugs to avoid being woken at night.

Quick self-check

  • Can I underline the topic sentence in each paragraph?
  • Does every sentence support that topic?
  • Have I given at least one clear example?
  • Would the paragraph still make sense on its own?
