Unit 13

Understanding the C1 Essay Task

Useful for:

  • Planning the C1 essay
  • Avoiding repetition of ideas

Moving from B2 to C1 is like upgrading from driving a small car to piloting a more powerful one. The controls are similar – you still need direction, balance, and focus – but everything responds faster.

At this level, examiners expect you not only to communicate clearly, but also to think and write flexibly. That means understanding the task, planning a clear structure, and then developing ideas with depth and balance.

In this unit, we will follow a simple four-step plan:

  • First, understand exactly what the C1 essay is and what is tested.
  • Second, see how it differs from the B2 essay.
  • Third, learn how to analyse the task and plan your structure.
  • Finally, practise writing and refining with formal, natural language.

1. What the C1 Essay Is

Before you can plan or write well, you need a clear picture of the task itself. In the Cambridge C1 Advanced (CAE) exam, Part 1 of the Writing paper is always an essay – just like at B2. However, the input is different.

Instead of three short notes, you are given two input texts such as short quotes, extracts, or opinions. You must read, understand, and use ideas from both.

Example task

In your English class, you have been discussing the topic of work and career.
Below are some opinions from your classmates:

“People should enjoy their job, not just work for money.”
“The most important thing is to have job security.”

Write an essay discussing both of these opinions and give your own view.
Write 220–260 words.

Everything else in this unit builds on this simple fact: you always work with two opinions + your view.

2. What’s Being Tested

Once you know what the task looks like, the next step in our plan is to see how it is marked. The essay is assessed using four criteria from the Cambridge Assessment Writing Scale:

  • Content – You include all parts of the task.
    You discuss both opinions and give your own view.
  • Communicative Achievement – You write in an appropriate, formal style.
    No slang, no contractions, clear argument.
  • Organisation – Your essay is logical and coherent.
    Smooth transitions, clear paragraphs.
  • Language – You show range and control of grammar and vocabulary.
    Complex sentences, advanced vocabulary, natural tone.

Our unit plan follows the same logic: first understand the task, then build a structure, then refine language and organisation.

3. The Key Difference Between B2 and C1

At B2, you show that you can organise your ideas and write clearly. At C1, you show that you can think – analyse, balance, and develop arguments with depth.

B2 vs C1 focus

  • Understanding
    B2: Follows instructions and covers notes.
    C1: Synthesises ideas from two sources.
  • Organisation
    B2: Logical, simple structure.
    C1: Flexible but coherent argument.
  • Language
    B2: Mostly accurate, clear grammar.
    C1: Range, control, and sophistication.
  • Tone
    B2: Formal and clear.
    C1: Formal and mature, with natural flow.
  • Word count
    B2: 140–190 words.
    C1: 220–260 words.

This step in our plan tells you why the C1 essay feels different: it asks for evaluation and balance, not just description.

4. How to Read and Analyse the Task

Now that you know what the exam wants, the next step is to read the question in a structured way. Before you write, take two minutes to read the task carefully.

You will always identify three elements:

  1. The topic (e.g. work and career).
  2. Two opinions or extracts – each expressing a different view.
  3. The instruction – for example: “Write an essay discussing both opinions and give your own view.”

Analysing our example

  • Topic: work and career
  • Opinions:
    1. “People should enjoy their job, not just work for money.”
    2. “The most important thing is to have job security.”
  • Task: Discuss both opinions and give your own.

Your essay must therefore include:

  • a clear introduction to the topic,
  • a discussion of both opinions, supported by examples,
  • a final paragraph expressing your own view.

Analysing the task in this way feeds directly into the next stage of our plan: building a clear structure.

5. The Recommended Structure

Although there are many possible essay structures, here is one clear overall plan that works perfectly for the C1 exam:

Part Job Approx. word count
1. Introduction Introduce the topic and mention both opinions. 40–50 words
2. Paragraph 1 Discuss the first opinion and give your evaluation. 60–70 words
3. Paragraph 2 Discuss the second opinion and give your evaluation. 60–70 words
4. Conclusion Present your own view and summarise logically. 40–50 words

This is the “map” for your essay. The rest of the unit will show you how to fill each part of the map with clear, non-repetitive content.

Planning your C1 essay

Before you start writing, spend two or three minutes turning the task into this structure:

  • Note one key idea for opinion 1.
  • Note one key idea for opinion 2.
  • Decide your final view for the conclusion.

A clear paragraph plan keeps your essay focused and stops you from repeating the same idea in different places.

6. Writing the Introduction

Once the plan is clear, you can start filling the first part: the introduction. At C1 level, it should sound calm, natural, and thoughtful.

Avoid personal phrases like I think or In my opinion in the first paragraph. You can save them for the conclusion, where you give your final view.

Example introduction

Choosing a career path is one of the most important decisions in life, and people’s priorities often differ. Some believe that job satisfaction should come first, while others argue that security and stability are more valuable. Both perspectives have merit and deserve consideration.

This introduction follows the plan: it names the topic (career), mentions both opinions, and sets up the discussion without giving your final answer yet.

7. Developing the Body Paragraphs

The next step is to develop each opinion in its own paragraph. You do not just describe it – you evaluate it and show advantages and limits.

Example (Paragraph 1: Enjoyment vs money)

On the one hand, enjoying one’s job is essential for long-term motivation. People who feel passionate about their work are usually more productive and creative. However, this approach can be risky if the job market is unstable or salaries are low, as enthusiasm alone cannot pay the bills.

Example (Paragraph 2: Job security)

On the other hand, having a secure job offers financial stability and peace of mind. A reliable income allows individuals to plan their future and support their families. Nevertheless, staying too long in an unfulfilling job can cause boredom and stress, which may eventually affect performance.

Each paragraph presents an idea, supports it, and then adds a balanced perspective. This is how you turn your plan into real content.

8. Writing the Conclusion

The conclusion is where your plan ends: you now give your opinion clearly, calmly, and logically. Avoid copying sentences from earlier paragraphs.

Example conclusion

In my view, while job security is important, genuine job satisfaction plays a greater role in long-term happiness. Ideally, people should aim for a balance between financial stability and personal fulfilment in their careers.

This conclusion fits the plan: it recognises both opinions and then gives a final, balanced answer to the question.

9. Formal Language and Register

With the structure in place, the next stage is to polish your language. At C1 level, the register becomes even more critical. You must write formally, but with a natural rhythm.

Avoid

  • Contractions: ❌ don’t, can’t, won’t
  • Slang: ❌ a lot of, really, kids, stuff
  • Personal anecdotes: ❌ I once had a job…
  • Emotional exaggeration: ❌ It’s totally amazing!

Use instead

  • It is widely believed that…
  • A common argument is that…
  • It could be argued that…
  • This view suggests that…
  • However / Nevertheless / In contrast…
  • To some extent / On the whole / In conclusion…

This step of the plan ensures that your well-structured essay also sounds mature and consistent.

10. Using More Sophisticated Connectors

While B2 essays use clear connectors (firstly, on the other hand, in conclusion), at C1 you can vary them for elegance and precision.

Function Common (B2) Advanced (C1)
Adding also, moreover furthermore, in addition, what is more
Contrasting but, however nevertheless, nonetheless, whereas, while
Result so, therefore as a result, consequently, thus
Emphasis really, very indeed, in fact, above all, particularly
Conclusion in conclusion to sum up, overall, in summary

You do not need to use them all – just choose the ones that support your plan and make your argument smoother.

11. Common Mistakes at C1 (and How to Fix Them)

Only discussing one opinion

Mistake: Only writing about job satisfaction.

Correction: Always include both viewpoints.

Overusing advanced words

Mistake: Individuals must contemplate occupational fulfilment paradigms.

Correction: Keep vocabulary advanced but natural: job satisfaction.

Repeating the same idea

At C1, many essays lose marks because they repeat the same argument with different words instead of developing it.

❌ Paragraph 1 and Paragraph 2 both say “job satisfaction is important” without adding new reasons.
✅ Paragraph 1 focuses on motivation and creativity, while Paragraph 2 focuses on mental health and long-term engagement.

When editing, ask: “Does this sentence add something new, or just repeat what I already said?” If it repeats, either cut it or deepen it. This keeps your overall plan strong and your paragraphs clearly distinct.

Too many long sentences

Mistake: Extremely long sentences with many clauses.

Correction: Mix long and short sentences for rhythm.

Personal tone

Mistake: I totally agree with the first opinion.

Correction: This perspective seems more convincing.

Ignoring the word limit

Mistake: Writing 300+ words.

Correction: Stay within 220–260 words.

Remember: clarity first, complexity second. You do not get higher marks for complex vocabulary – only for using it correctly and in line with your plan.

12. The Balance Between Formal and Natural

Advanced writing does not mean robotic writing. You still want your essay to sound human and readable.

Compare

Too formal (overwritten):
Individuals ought to pursue occupational satisfaction insofar as it contributes to their overall psychological well-being.

Natural, formal, clear:
People should seek job satisfaction because it contributes to mental well-being.

The second is simpler – and stronger. At C1, simplicity with control beats complexity without clarity.

13. A Full Model Essay (≈ 240 words)

Task

Write an essay discussing both opinions and give your own view.
Topic: Work and career.

Model essay

People often disagree about what makes a job worthwhile. Some believe that enjoyment should come first, while others argue that security and stability are more important.

On the one hand, job satisfaction is crucial for long-term success. People who enjoy what they do tend to perform better and feel more fulfilled. For example, those who work in creative fields often find meaning and motivation in their tasks, which increases their productivity. However, pursuing only passion can sometimes lead to financial difficulties or instability.

On the other hand, job security provides peace of mind and financial confidence. A reliable income allows individuals to plan their future and support their families. Nevertheless, staying too long in an unfulfilling job can cause boredom and stress, which may eventually affect performance.

In my opinion, the best approach is balance. While financial stability is essential, genuine satisfaction gives life purpose. Ideally, people should seek careers that combine both enjoyment and security, even if this means making compromises along the way.

Why it fits the plan

  • Discusses both opinions equally.
  • Follows the four-part structure clearly.
  • Uses formal but readable language.
  • Ends with a balanced final view.
  • Stays within the word limit.

This is precisely the kind of essay that earns Band 3–4 (C1) marks in the Cambridge Writing Paper.

14. Final Reflection

The C1 essay is not about writing longer or using bigger words. It is about thinking deeply, organising clearly, and expressing yourself with balance and confidence.

B2 shows clarity.
C1 shows control.

At this level, you do not prove your knowledge by complexity – you prove it by precision. You have already built strong writing habits in the previous units. Now, the goal is refinement – polishing your voice into one that sounds natural, thoughtful, and complete.
