Unit 15

Writing and Linking C1 Paragraphs

By now, you know how to analyse a C1 essay question and create a logical plan. You can identify the two opinions, form your own view, and clearly map out your ideas.

Now it’s time to turn that plan into writing — not just sentences, but paragraphs that flow, connect, and sound mature.

This unit will teach you how to write C1-level paragraphs that:

  • express complex ideas clearly,
  • use logical and stylistic linking,
  • maintain a calm, natural rhythm.

In other words, we’ll learn how to move beyond structure and into flow.

1. What Makes a C1 Paragraph Different

At B2 level, a good paragraph is one clear idea with a few supporting sentences. At C1 level, the same rule applies — but you need to go deeper.

C1 paragraphs show:

  • Balance — they present an idea and acknowledge other perspectives.
  • Development — they include examples, explanations, and mini-conclusions.
  • Variety — they use a mix of sentence structures for rhythm.
  • Cohesion — ideas link smoothly to what comes before and after.

In short:
B2 paragraphs explain. C1 paragraphs explore.

2. Structure: The “Deep PEEL” Model

At this level, we expand the familiar model (Point – Evidence – Explain – Link) into a more flexible version:

PEEL

P – Point
Introduce the main idea clearly.
Job satisfaction improves motivation.

E – Evidence
Add support (example, fact, or situation).
For example, people who enjoy their work often perform better.

E – Explain
Show why the idea is true or important.
This is because enjoyment increases creativity and persistence.

L – Link
Connect to the essay’s main question or next paragraph.
Nevertheless, not everyone has the luxury of choosing a job they love.

C1 Extension: Evaluate (E²)

Offer a balanced or reflective comment.
Therefore, while satisfaction is desirable, stability may sometimes take priority.

This final “Evaluate” stage is what examiners notice. It shows critical thinking.

3. Example: Deep PEEL in Action

Topic: Technology and convenience.

Paragraph Example:

One clear advantage of modern technology is that it has made communication faster and more convenient. For instance, online platforms allow people to stay in contact regardless of distance. This saves both time and money, making it easier to maintain friendships or work relationships. However, this constant availability can also increase stress, as people feel pressure to respond immediately. Overall, while technology has improved communication, it has also blurred the line between work and personal life.

Why this works:

  • It begins clearly.
  • It gives examples.
  • It shows understanding of both sides.
  • It ends with a reflective evaluation.

This is what examiners mean by “shows awareness of complexity.”

4. Linking Within a Paragraph

At C1, sentences must flow naturally. Use linking with a light touch.

(a) Logical connectors (subtle and natural)

Use them sparingly.

FunctionExamples
Additionmoreover, in addition, what is more
Contrasthowever, nevertheless, yet, whereas
Cause/resulttherefore, thus, consequently, as a result
Examplefor instance, for example, such as
Emphasisindeed, in fact, above all, particularly

Avoid overuse — too many connectors sound mechanical.

(b) Reference words

Pronouns create smooth flow.

  • This change affects how people work, communicate, and relax.
  • Such initiatives are essential for the future.

(c) Thematic repetition

Use synonyms to keep unity:

education → learning → schooling → academic development

(d) Sentence variety

Mix short and long sentences for rhythm.

5. Linking Between Paragraphs

B2 uses simple transitions.
C1 uses subtle logical bridges.

(a) Transitional Phrases

Paragraph FunctionUseful Starters
First argumentTo begin with…
Opposing viewNevertheless…
Further developmentFurthermore…
ConclusionOverall…

(b) Logical Echo

Use a key idea from the previous paragraph (in a new form) at the start of the next one. This transformation pulls the reader forward.

(c) The Contrast Bridge

End one paragraph with a “but” idea, start the next with the continuation: the reader feels the connection without you repeating everything.

(d) Balance Signal

Use phrases that show nuanced thought, such as While this may be true in some cases… or Although this argument is convincing…

6. Developing Paragraph Depth

(a) Cause and Effect

Use clear cause–effect chains to deepen ideas: Because many people work from home, they may struggle to separate work and personal life, which can lead to stress.

(b) Consequences

Show “what happens next”: As a result, productivity may actually fall, despite longer working hours.

(c) Qualification

Add “but” ideas that create nuance: However, this is not always the case for people who set clear limits.

7. Building Sentence Flexibility

Examiners look for variety in structure:

  • Simple: Technology changes quickly.
  • Compound: Technology changes quickly, and many people struggle to keep up.
  • Complex: Although technology changes quickly, education systems often respond slowly.
  • Compound–Complex: Although technology changes quickly, education systems often respond slowly, and this can widen the skills gap.

Mixing these types makes your paragraphs feel more natural and sophisticated.

8. Example: Linked C1 Paragraphs in Full

Paragraph 1 (advantages):

It is clear that modern technology has simplified many aspects of life. Daily tasks such as shopping, banking, and studying can now be completed online in minutes. This increased efficiency saves time and allows people to focus on other priorities. In addition, technology supports accessibility by helping those with disabilities to work or communicate more easily.

Link to Paragraph 2:
Nevertheless, convenience often comes at a psychological cost.

Paragraph 2 (disadvantages):

Constant connectivity can lead to fatigue and distraction. For example, people frequently check their phones even during conversations, which reduces attention and social quality. Moreover, the pressure to be always available creates anxiety and disrupts rest. Therefore, while technology has made life faster, it has also made it harder for people to disconnect.

Conclusion:

In my view, technology has improved life overall, but individuals must learn to set boundaries.

9. Language of Balance and Nuance

Useful “C1 feel” phrases include:

  • To some extent…
  • Although this may be the case…
  • It could be said that…
  • While it is true that…
  • On balance…

These small expressions show that you can see more than one side of an argument.

10. Common Paragraph Mistakes at C1

  • No clear topic sentence – the reader doesn’t know what the paragraph is about.
  • Too many ideas in one paragraph – several points are mentioned but none are developed.
  • Repetition of connectors – every sentence begins with “Firstly, Secondly, Thirdly…”.
  • Overly long sentences – ideas get lost and errors increase.
  • Personal tone – too much “I think” or informal language in a formal essay.

11. Practice: Rewrite and Link

Exercise:

People like using technology. They can work faster. It saves time. But they use phones too much. It can make people stressed. They need to relax more.

Possible answer:

Technology helps people work more efficiently and saves valuable time in daily life. Online tools, for example, allow tasks that once took hours to be completed in seconds.

However, excessive use of technology can lead to stress. Many people spend too much time on their phones, which reduces real rest and concentration. Therefore, balance is essential: technology should serve people, not control them.

12. Editing for Flow

When you re-read your essay, ask yourself:

  • Does each paragraph have a clear focus?
  • Do sentences flow naturally?
  • Is there variety in structure?
  • Does each paragraph connect logically to the next?

If you can answer “yes” to these questions, your paragraphs are already working at C1 level.

13. Final Reflection

At C1 level, paragraphs are small journeys — each with direction, rhythm, and reflection.

B2 writers connect ideas.
C1 writers connect thinking.
